The empirical studies presented in Chapters~\ref{chp:pinning}, \ref{chp:fake-stars}, and~\ref{chp:cursor} demonstrate how a pragmatic causal inference approach---one that makes theory and assumptions explicit, acknowledges methodological limitations honestly, and navigates alternative explanations thoughtfully---can build trustworthy evidence to challenge widely held software engineering beliefs.
In each case, conventional wisdom---that dependency pinning improves security, that artificial popularity signals provide sustainable promotional benefits, and that AI coding assistants unambiguously improve productivity---was examined through causal analysis with transparently stated assumptions.
These findings illustrate the central thesis: When software engineering myths are subjected to appropriate methodological scrutiny, with clear articulation of identifying assumptions and honest acknowledgment of scope conditions, we can build credible evidence that sometimes contradicts practitioners' expectations.

Building on these contributions, this chapter outlines proposed work that extends the thesis in three complementary directions---each designed to further demonstrate the value and practice of pragmatic causal inference.
First, I propose to strengthen the causal evidence in the fake GitHub stars study (Chapter~\ref{chp:fake-stars}) by applying panel generalized method of moments (GMM) analysis, addressing potential endogeneity concerns in the current panel autoregression approach and illustrating how method selection should respond to specific threats to validity (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-gmm}).
Second, I propose a systematic sensitivity analysis comparing difference-in-differences with alternative analytical approaches in the Cursor study (Chapter~\ref{chp:cursor}), demonstrating concretely how the choice of analytical method affects conclusions and why pragmatic causal inference yields more credible findings than descriptive or correlational alternatives (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-sensitivity}).
Third, I propose revisiting the longstanding debate on programming languages and defect proneness~\cite{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14, DBLP:journals/cacm/RayPDF17, DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19}, applying the pragmatic causal inference framework to a contested question that has resisted consensus despite extensive prior research---thereby demonstrating the generalizability of this approach (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-language}).
Together, these proposed contributions aim to provide both empirical insights and a methodological template for navigating the trade-offs inherent in causal inference, illustrating how to build trustworthy evidence incrementally through complementary designs with explicitly stated assumptions.

\section{Strengthening Causal Evidence in the Fake Star Study}
\label{sec:proposed-gmm}

\subsection{Motivation and Limitations of the Current Study}

The fake GitHub stars study (Chapter~\ref{chp:fake-stars}) provides substantial evidence regarding the prevalence, characteristics, and short-term ineffectiveness of fake star campaigns.
Specifically, the RQ4 analysis employs panel autoregression models to estimate the promotional effect of fake stars, finding that fake stars have a modest positive effect on attracting real stars in the short term (one to two months) but become a liability in the long term.
While these findings are consistent across multiple model specifications (Table~\ref{tab:all-regressions}) and provide initial evidence against the growth hacking hypothesis, the current analysis has several methodological limitations that warrant additional investigation.

First, the panel autoregression approach assumes that the relationship between fake stars and subsequent organic growth is unidirectional: repositories acquire fake stars, which then influences their ability to attract real stars.
However, this assumption may be violated in practice.
Repositories experiencing organic growth may also be more likely to purchase fake stars to amplify momentum, creating a potential reverse causality problem.
The current study acknowledges this limitation but does not rigorously address it.

Second, while the panel autoregression models include fixed effects to control for time-invariant repository characteristics, they do not fully account for time-varying confounders that may influence both fake star acquisition and organic growth.
For example, external promotional activities (e.g., social media campaigns, conference presentations, or product launches) may coincide with fake star purchases, and these unobserved time-varying factors could bias the estimated effects.

Third, the current analysis relies on the assumption that lagged values of fake and real stars are exogenous after conditioning on fixed effects.
This assumption, while common in panel data analysis, may be violated if there exist dynamic feedback loops between variables that are not fully captured by the specified lag structure.

\subsection{Proposed Analysis: Panel GMM Estimation}

To address these limitations, I propose applying the Arellano-Bond dynamic panel generalized method of moments (GMM) estimator~\cite{arellano1991some}, which provides consistent estimates when explanatory variables are potentially endogenous.
This approach has been successfully applied in Chapter~\ref{chp:cursor} to disentangle the temporal interactions between development velocity and software quality; applying the same methodology to the fake stars study would strengthen the causal evidence and enable more direct comparisons across the two empirical contexts.

Specifically, I propose estimating the following dynamic panel model:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:proposed-gmm-fake}
\textit{real}_{i,t} = \mu_i + \lambda_t + \rho \cdot \textit{real}_{i,t-1} + \beta \cdot \textit{fake}_{i,t} + \Gamma' Z_{i,t} + \epsilon_{i,t}
\end{equation}
where $\textit{real}_{i,t}$ represents the count of real (non-fake) stars received by repository $i$ in month $t$; $\textit{fake}_{i,t}$ represents the count of fake stars acquired in month $t$; $\mu_i$ and $\lambda_t$ are repository and time fixed effects, respectively; $Z_{i,t}$ includes time-varying control variables (age, release status, authentic development activity); and $\epsilon_{i,t}$ is the error term.

The key innovation of GMM estimation lies in its treatment of potentially endogenous regressors.
Rather than assuming that $\textit{fake}_{i,t}$ is exogenous, the GMM approach uses lagged values of the endogenous variables as instrumental variables.
Under the assumption that $\textit{fake}_{i,t-2}$, $\textit{fake}_{i,t-3}$, etc., are correlated with current fake stars but uncorrelated with the current error term (conditional on fixed effects), these lagged values provide valid instruments for identification.

The validity of this approach rests on two testable assumptions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=15pt]
    \item \textbf{Instrument validity}: The lagged values must be uncorrelated with the contemporaneous error term, testable via the Sargan test of overidentifying restrictions.
    \item \textbf{No serial correlation in the original errors}: While the first-differenced errors will exhibit first-order autocorrelation by construction, there should be no second-order autocorrelation, testable via the AR(2) test.
\end{enumerate}

Passing these specification tests would provide stronger evidence that the estimated effects represent genuine causal relationships rather than spurious correlations arising from endogeneity.

\subsection{Expected Contributions}

The proposed GMM analysis is expected to contribute in three ways.
First, it will provide more rigorous causal evidence regarding the promotional (in)effectiveness of fake stars.
If the GMM estimates confirm the panel autoregression findings---that fake stars provide only transient benefits and become long-term liabilities---this would substantially strengthen the main conclusions of Chapter~\ref{chp:fake-stars}.
Alternatively, if GMM reveals different effect magnitudes or patterns, this would indicate that endogeneity bias affected the original estimates, providing important methodological insights.

Second, the proposed analysis enables a direct methodological comparison between panel autoregression and panel GMM within the same empirical context.
This comparison will illuminate when and why the choice of estimation method matters for causal inference in software engineering research.

Third, this analysis demonstrates the generalizability of the GMM approach introduced in Chapter~\ref{chp:cursor} to different empirical settings within software engineering.
By applying the same methodological framework across two distinct studies---one examining tool adoption effects on code quality (Cursor) and another examining promotional signal manipulation (fake stars)---the thesis establishes GMM as a broadly applicable technique for addressing endogeneity concerns in longitudinal software repository data.

\section{Sensitivity Analysis: Comparing DiD with Alternative Designs}
\label{sec:proposed-sensitivity}

\subsection{Motivation: The Importance of Causal Identification}

The Cursor study (Chapter~\ref{chp:cursor}) employs a difference-in-differences (DiD) design with staggered adoption to estimate the causal effect of Cursor adoption on development velocity and software quality.
The study finds that Cursor adoption leads to transient velocity gains but persistent increases in technical debt, contradicting the prevailing narrative that AI coding assistants unambiguously improve developer productivity.
These findings have important implications for practitioners, tool designers, and researchers.

However, the study's conclusions depend critically on the methodological choice to employ DiD rather than simpler correlational or descriptive analyses.
While Chapter~\ref{chp:cursor} provides extensive robustness checks comparing different DiD estimators (two-way fixed effects, Callaway and Sant'Anna, Borusyak imputation), it does not systematically compare DiD with fundamentally different analytical approaches.
This leaves open the question: \emph{Would the study have reached the same conclusions using descriptive or correlational methods commonly employed in empirical software engineering research?}

This question is not merely academic.
If DiD and simpler methods yield similar conclusions, one might argue that the additional methodological complexity is unnecessary.
Conversely, if DiD yields substantially different---and more credible---conclusions, this would demonstrate the practical importance of pragmatic causal inference and motivate its broader adoption in the field.

\subsection{Proposed Analysis: Systematic Methodological Comparison}

I propose a systematic sensitivity analysis that re-analyzes the Cursor adoption data using a hierarchy of analytical approaches, ranging from purely descriptive statistics to sophisticated causal inference designs.
Specifically, I will implement and compare the following approaches:

\paragraph{Approach 1: Descriptive Comparison}
The simplest approach compares outcome means between Cursor-adopting repositories and non-adopting repositories without adjusting for potential confounders.
This approach, while naive, represents a baseline that practitioners might employ when evaluating tool effectiveness based on observational data.
Formally, the descriptive estimator is:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\tau}_{\text{desc}} = \bar{Y}_{\text{treated}} - \bar{Y}_{\text{control}}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{Y}_{\text{treated}}$ and $\bar{Y}_{\text{control}}$ are the mean outcomes in the treated and control groups, respectively, computed over all post-adoption observations.

\paragraph{Approach 2: Cross-Sectional Regression}
A slightly more sophisticated approach uses cross-sectional regression to control for observable differences between treated and control repositories.
This approach, common in empirical software engineering, estimates:
\begin{equation}
Y_{it} = \alpha + \beta D_{it} + \Gamma' Z_{it} + \epsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
where $D_{it}$ is a treatment indicator and $Z_{it}$ includes time-varying covariates (lines of code, contributors, stars, issues).
The coefficient $\hat{\beta}$ estimates the treatment effect conditional on observables.

\paragraph{Approach 3: One-Way Fixed Effects (Repository Only)}
Adding repository fixed effects controls for time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity:
\begin{equation}
Y_{it} = \mu_i + \beta D_{it} + \Gamma' Z_{it} + \epsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
This approach, often described as ``controlling for project characteristics,'' is frequently employed in longitudinal software repository analyses.
However, it does not account for time-varying confounders common to all repositories.

\paragraph{Approach 4: One-Way Fixed Effects (Time Only)}
Alternatively, time fixed effects control for macro trends affecting all repositories:
\begin{equation}
Y_{it} = \lambda_t + \beta D_{it} + \Gamma' Z_{it} + \epsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
This approach accounts for factors such as changes in GitHub platform features, seasonal patterns in open-source activity, or evolving LLM capabilities.

\paragraph{Approach 5: Two-Way Fixed Effects (TWFE)}
Combining both repository and time fixed effects yields the TWFE estimator, a standard approach in panel data analysis:
\begin{equation}
Y_{it} = \mu_i + \lambda_t + \beta D_{it} + \Gamma' Z_{it} + \epsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
As discussed in Chapter~\ref{chp:cursor}, TWFE can produce biased estimates under staggered adoption with heterogeneous treatment effects.

\paragraph{Approach 6: Modern DiD Estimators}
Finally, the Borusyak imputation estimator and Callaway-Sant'Anna estimator represent state-of-the-art approaches designed explicitly for staggered adoption settings.
These methods avoid the ``forbidden comparisons'' that can bias TWFE estimates and provide valid inference under weaker assumptions.

\subsection{Comparison Framework}

For each approach, I will report:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=15pt]
    \item \textbf{Point estimates and confidence intervals}: The estimated treatment effects on each outcome (commits, lines added, static analysis warnings, duplicate line density, code complexity).
    \item \textbf{Statistical significance}: Whether the estimated effects are statistically distinguishable from zero at conventional significance levels.
    \item \textbf{Sign and magnitude}: Whether the qualitative conclusions (e.g., velocity increase, quality decrease) are consistent across approaches.
    \item \textbf{Temporal dynamics}: Whether the approaches capture the transient nature of velocity gains and the persistence of quality degradation.
\end{enumerate}

Additionally, I will assess the \emph{plausibility} of each approach by examining:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=15pt]
    \item \textbf{Pre-trend tests}: For approaches that support pre-trend analysis (DiD variants), whether parallel trends hold.
    \item \textbf{Covariate balance}: For approaches involving comparison groups, whether treatment and control groups are comparable on observable characteristics.
    \item \textbf{Specification tests}: For approaches with testable assumptions (e.g., Sargan test for GMM), whether the assumptions are satisfied.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Expected Findings and Contributions}

Based on preliminary intuitions and prior research on the limitations of correlational analysis~\cite{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19}, I hypothesize that:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=15pt]
    \item Descriptive comparisons and cross-sectional regressions will overestimate the positive effects of Cursor adoption on velocity, as they fail to account for selection bias (more active repositories may be more likely to adopt Cursor).
    \item Approaches without time fixed effects will confound Cursor effects with broader trends in open-source development activity.
    \item TWFE may produce attenuated or biased estimates due to negative weighting from ``forbidden comparisons'' under treatment effect heterogeneity.
    \item Modern DiD estimators will reveal the transient nature of velocity gains and the persistence of quality degradation that simpler methods obscure.
\end{enumerate}

If confirmed, these hypotheses would demonstrate that \emph{the choice of analytical method materially affects conclusions about AI tool effectiveness}.
This finding would carry important implications for the interpretation of prior empirical studies on AI coding assistants, many of which rely on correlational evidence or simple before-after comparisons.
More broadly, the proposed analysis would serve as a methodological case study illustrating why pragmatic causal inference---with explicit assumptions, diagnostic tests, and honest acknowledgment of limitations---yields more credible findings than approaches that either ignore causality entirely or make implicit assumptions that cannot be examined.

The expected contributions of this sensitivity analysis are threefold.
First, it will provide a concrete demonstration of how different analytical approaches can lead to different conclusions on the same data, reinforcing the thesis argument that methodological rigor matters for myth-busting research.
Second, it will offer practical guidance for researchers selecting among alternative analytical approaches, highlighting the trade-offs between simplicity and validity.
Third, it will establish a template for systematic methodological comparison that can be applied in future empirical software engineering studies.

\section{Revisiting the Programming Language and Defect Proneness Debate}
\label{sec:proposed-language}

\subsection{Background: A Longstanding Controversy}

The question of whether programming language choice causally affects software defect rates represents one of the most contentious debates in empirical software engineering.
The seminal study by \citet{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14}, published at FSE 2014 and later extended in Communications of the ACM~\cite{DBLP:journals/cacm/RayPDF17}, analyzed millions of commits across thousands of GitHub projects and concluded that programming language has a significant but modest effect on defect proneness.
Specifically, the study found that functional languages (Haskell, Scala, Clojure) and statically-typed languages (Java, C\#, C++) are associated with fewer defects compared to dynamically-typed languages (JavaScript, Ruby, Python).

However, a rigorous reproduction study by \citet{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19} challenged these conclusions on methodological grounds.
The reproduction identified several issues with the original study: errors in language classification, inadequate handling of polyglot repositories, and the use of statistical models inappropriate for the data structure.
After correcting these issues, the reproduction found that many of the original effects shrank dramatically or failed to replicate entirely.

The ensuing debate~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-07393, DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-11894} crystallized around a fundamental methodological question: Can observational analysis of GitHub data ever establish causal claims about the effect of programming language on defect proneness?
The critics argued that such analysis ``is necessarily measuring correlation and not establishing causation, as it does not constitute an experiment''~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-11894}.

This debate exemplifies the broader problem motivating this thesis: the field's inability to reach consensus on empirical questions due to methodological limitations.
Both the original study and the reproduction relied fundamentally on correlational analysis, even when sophisticated statistical controls were employed.
Neither study employed identification strategies that would warrant causal interpretation under explicitly stated assumptions.
I propose to revisit this debate using the causal inference lens developed throughout this thesis.

\subsection{Proposed Analysis: Identification and Control Strategies}

The fundamental challenge in establishing causal claims about programming language effects is that language choice is not random.
Developers select languages based on project requirements, personal expertise, team composition, organizational mandates, and ecosystem maturity---factors that may independently affect defect rates.
Simple comparisons between projects using different languages will therefore conflate language effects with selection effects.

I propose a two-pronged approach that combines control strategies to address observable confounding with identification strategies that exploit quasi-experimental variation.

\subsubsection{Control Strategy: Developer and Repository Fixed Effects}

The first component of my approach leverages the panel structure of software repository data to control for unobserved heterogeneity at both the developer and repository levels.
The key insight is that many developers and repositories work with multiple programming languages over time, creating within-unit variation that can be exploited for identification.

Specifically, I propose estimating fixed effects models of the form:
\begin{equation}
\textit{Defects}_{ijt} = \alpha_i + \gamma_j + \lambda_t + \beta \cdot \textit{Language}_{ijt} + \Gamma' Z_{ijt} + \epsilon_{ijt}
\end{equation}
where $\textit{Defects}_{ijt}$ represents defect-related commits by developer $i$ in repository $j$ at time $t$; $\alpha_i$ represents developer fixed effects capturing time-invariant developer characteristics (skill level, coding style, attention to quality); $\gamma_j$ represents repository fixed effects capturing time-invariant project characteristics (complexity, domain, team culture); $\lambda_t$ represents time fixed effects capturing temporal trends; and $\textit{Language}_{ijt}$ indicates the programming language used.

This specification controls for confounders that are constant within developers (e.g., expertise) or within repositories (e.g., project complexity).
The identifying variation comes from developers who contribute to multiple languages or repositories that use multiple languages, enabling within-unit comparisons that are less susceptible to selection bias than between-unit comparisons.

However, fixed effects alone cannot address time-varying confounders or selection on unobservables.
A developer who switches from JavaScript to TypeScript may do so precisely because they are working on a more complex feature requiring stronger typing---in which case the language switch is confounded with feature complexity.
This motivates the second component of my approach.

\subsubsection{Identification Strategy: Natural Experiments on Language Migration}

The second component exploits quasi-experimental variation arising from language migration events.
Specifically, I propose to identify repositories that underwent systematic language migrations---transitions from one primary language to another---and use these events as natural experiments for estimating language effects.

The rationale is as follows: When a repository migrates from Language A to Language B (e.g., from JavaScript to TypeScript), the migration decision is endogenous and cannot be treated as random.
However, the \emph{timing} of migration may exhibit quasi-random variation conditional on the decision to migrate.
Some repositories migrate early in the observation period; others migrate late.
This staggered timing creates variation that can be exploited using the difference-in-differences framework developed in Chapter~\ref{chp:cursor}.

Formally, let $E_j$ denote the time of language migration for repository $j$.
I propose estimating:
\begin{equation}
\textit{Defects}_{jt} = \gamma_j + \lambda_t + \sum_{h=-k}^{m} \tau_h \cdot \mathbf{1}[t = E_j + h] + \Gamma' Z_{jt} + \epsilon_{jt}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_h$ represents the treatment effect $h$ periods relative to migration.
For $h < 0$, these estimates serve as pre-trend tests; for $h \geq 0$, they estimate the dynamic effect of language migration on defect rates.

This design requires several assumptions for causal interpretation:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=15pt]
    \item \textbf{Parallel trends}: Absent migration, repositories that migrate early would have followed similar defect trajectories as repositories that migrate late.
    \item \textbf{No anticipation}: Defect rates do not change in anticipation of migration (testable via pre-trend analysis).
    \item \textbf{Exogenous timing}: Conditional on the decision to migrate, the timing of migration is not correlated with time-varying determinants of defect rates.
\end{enumerate}

While these assumptions cannot be directly verified, they can be assessed through diagnostic tests (pre-trend analysis, placebo tests) and robustness checks (varying the migration definition, excluding borderline cases).

\subsubsection{Case Study: JavaScript to TypeScript Migration}

As a concrete instantiation of this approach, I propose focusing on the JavaScript to TypeScript migration, which offers several methodological advantages.
First, TypeScript is a strict superset of JavaScript, enabling gradual adoption that creates rich within-repository variation.
Second, the migration is observable through file extensions and compilation configurations, enabling reliable identification of migration events.
Third, the JavaScript/TypeScript comparison directly tests the hypothesis that static typing reduces defects---one of the central claims in the original language study~\cite{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14}.
Fourth, JavaScript and TypeScript are the most common languages in the Cursor study dataset (Chapter~\ref{chp:cursor}), providing a natural connection between the proposed and completed work.

I will operationalize the analysis as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=15pt]
    \item \textbf{Identify migration events}: For each repository, track the proportion of code in TypeScript versus JavaScript over time. Define migration as a sustained shift from majority-JavaScript to majority-TypeScript.
    \item \textbf{Construct treatment and comparison groups}: Treated repositories are those that undergo migration during the observation period; comparison repositories are those that remain JavaScript-dominant throughout.
    \item \textbf{Measure defect outcomes}: Operationalize defects using bug-fix commits (identified via commit message patterns~\cite{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14}), static analysis warnings (using the SonarQube infrastructure from Chapter~\ref{chp:cursor}), or issue reports labeled as bugs.
    \item \textbf{Apply DiD estimation}: Estimate the effect of migration using the Borusyak imputation estimator, which has shown robust performance in Chapter~\ref{chp:cursor}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Expected Challenges and Limitations}

The proposed analysis faces several challenges that must be acknowledged.
First, language migration is endogenous.
Repositories migrate for reasons (e.g., perceived quality benefits, ecosystem maturity, team expertise) that may independently affect defect rates.
While the DiD design addresses some selection concerns, it cannot fully eliminate confounding from time-varying factors correlated with both migration timing and defect rates.

Second, the parallel trends assumption may be violated if migrating repositories are on different trajectories than non-migrating repositories before migration.
Pre-trend tests can assess plausibility but cannot definitively validate the assumption.

Third, the generalizability of findings from JavaScript/TypeScript migration to broader language comparisons is limited.
TypeScript and JavaScript share syntax and runtime, making migration relatively seamless compared to more drastic language changes (e.g., from C++ to Rust).

Fourth, defining and measuring ``defects'' in software repositories remains methodologically challenging.
Bug-fix commits may be noisy proxies for actual defects; static analysis warnings include false positives; issue labels are inconsistently applied.

Despite these limitations, the proposed analysis represents a substantial methodological advance over prior work.
By combining control strategies (fixed effects) with identification strategies (DiD on migration events), the analysis provides stronger warrants for causal inference than purely correlational approaches.
Moreover, the explicit articulation of identifying assumptions enables transparent evaluation of the analysis's strengths and limitations.

\subsection{Expected Contributions}

The proposed analysis on programming language and defect proneness is expected to contribute in several ways.
First, it will provide new empirical evidence on a longstanding and consequential question, using methods that address the fundamental criticism leveled at prior work---that correlational analysis cannot establish causation.

Second, it will demonstrate how the causal inference methods developed throughout this thesis (fixed effects, DiD, GMM) can be applied to questions beyond tool adoption and signal manipulation.
This demonstrates the generalizability of the methodological framework and provides a template for future research on contested software engineering questions.

Third, the analysis will produce a concrete comparison between correlational and causal inference approaches on the same data, complementing the proposed sensitivity analysis on the Cursor study (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-sensitivity}).
Together, these analyses will provide compelling evidence for the thesis argument that pragmatic causal inference---with its emphasis on transparent assumptions and honest limitations---yields different and more trustworthy findings than descriptive or correlational approaches.

Finally, regardless of whether the analysis finds that programming language causally affects defect proneness, the methodological contribution stands.
If the analysis finds effects consistent with prior work~\cite{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14}, it would provide stronger warrants for the claimed relationships.
If the analysis finds null effects consistent with the reproduction~\cite{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19}, it would suggest that prior findings were indeed artifacts of confounding.
Either way, the field benefits from a more rigorous treatment of a consequential question.

\section{Summary}

This chapter has outlined three proposed contributions that extend the thesis in complementary directions, each illustrating a different facet of pragmatic causal inference in software engineering research.
The GMM analysis for the fake stars study (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-gmm}) addresses potential endogeneity concerns in the current panel autoregression approach, demonstrating how method selection should respond to specific threats to validity and how strengthening causal evidence involves iteratively addressing identified limitations.
The systematic methodological comparison for the Cursor study (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-sensitivity}) provides concrete evidence that the choice of analytical method materially affects conclusions, illustrating why pragmatic causal inference---with its emphasis on explicit assumptions and diagnostic tests---yields more credible findings than descriptive or correlational approaches.
The programming language study (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-language}) applies the pragmatic causal inference framework to a longstanding contested question, demonstrating the generalizability of the approach while honestly acknowledging the challenges and limitations inherent in any observational study of language effects.

Together with the completed empirical studies, these proposed contributions demonstrate that \emph{a pragmatic causal inference approach---one that makes theory and assumptions explicit, acknowledges methodological limitations honestly, and navigates alternative explanations thoughtfully---can build trustworthy evidence to dispel software engineering myths and inform evidence-based practice}.
By providing both empirical insights and methodological guidance that emphasizes transparency over perfection, this thesis aims to contribute to the transformation of software engineering into a more evidence-based discipline---not through claims of epistemological certainty, but through the honest accumulation of credible evidence across complementary designs.
