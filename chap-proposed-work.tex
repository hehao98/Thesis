The three empirical studies presented in Chapters~\ref{chp:pinning}, \ref{chp:fake-stars}, and~\ref{chp:cursor} demonstrate the feasibility of explicit causal credibility assessments in software engineering by applying the four-step process---making causal theory explicit, defining the estimand and identifying assumptions, honestly acknowledging limitations, and navigating alternative explanations---to produce trustworthy evidence that challenges widely held software engineering myths.
In each study, the four-step process guided the research design from the outset, ensuring that causal claims were appropriately calibrated to the strength of the evidence.

Building on these contributions, the proposed work extends the thesis by developing the causal credibility assessment framework into an accessible \emph{tutorial} aimed at the broader software engineering research community.
A key barrier to the adoption of explicit causal reasoning in software engineering is the lack of pedagogical materials that demonstrate how to apply causal frameworks to concrete, familiar research problems.
To address this, I propose a tutorial paper that uses the longstanding debate on whether programming language choice affects software defect proneness~\cite{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14, DBLP:journals/cacm/RayPDF17, DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19}---a controversy introduced in Chapter~\ref{chp:intro} and used as a running example throughout Chapter~\ref{chp:background}---as a worked example to walk readers through applying the four-step process to \emph{existing} empirical research.
The tutorial first applies the four-step framework to the debate surrounding the seminal study by \citet{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14} (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-assessment}), showing how explicit causal reasoning can clarify what existing evidence supports and where specific study designs would yield stronger evidence.
Then, informed by the assessment, I propose a panel fixed-effects analysis exploiting within-developer and within-repository variation as a concrete illustration of how the framework guides the design of future studies (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-fe}).
Together, these proposed contributions aim to improve the accessibility of the causal credibility assessment framework and further demonstrate its feasibility---not only for designing new studies but also for constructively engaging with existing research.

\section{Revisiting the Programming Language and Defect Proneness Debate}
\label{sec:proposed-language}

\subsection{Background: A Longstanding Controversy}

The question of whether programming language choice causally affects software defect rates is among the most contentious debates in empirical software engineering.
The seminal study by \citet{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14}, published at FSE 2014 and later extended in Communications of the ACM~\cite{DBLP:journals/cacm/RayPDF17}, analyzed 729 projects across 17 programming languages on GitHub and reported that certain language classes---such as functional and statically typed languages---are associated with fewer defect-fixing commits than other classes.
Importantly, the original authors were careful to frame their findings as correlational associations, describing the language effect as ``small but significant'' while acknowledging limitations of their observational design.

A reproduction study by \citet{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19} challenged both the robustness and the interpretation of these findings.
The reproduction identified errors in language classification, inadequate handling of polyglot repositories, and concerns about the statistical models employed.
After correcting these issues, the reproduction found that many of the original effects shrank dramatically or failed to replicate entirely.

The ensuing debate~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-07393, DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-11894} exposed a deeper methodological impasse.
The reproduction team argued that an ``analysis of GitHub is necessarily measuring correlation and not establishing causation, as it does not constitute an experiment''~\cite{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-11894}, while the original authors maintained that their statistical findings are robust despite corrections.
It is worth noting that the two groups disagree strongly on measurement---how to classify languages, handle polyglot repositories, and operationalize defect-proneness---and these measurement concerns are legitimate and important.
However, poor measurement is orthogonal to the causal reasoning problem addressed in this thesis, albeit also a very challenging and consequential topic in software engineering research that deserves separate treatment.

Crucially, neither group explicitly discusses the problem through the lens of causal reasoning.
The reproduction team's critique that observational data ``cannot establish causation'' is correct as a general statement, but it does not engage with the more productive question of \emph{under which specific conditions} observational data could or could not support causal interpretation, nor does it articulate what specific confounders or alternative causal structures threaten the findings.
Similarly, the original authors' defense focuses on the robustness of their statistical associations without articulating the assumptions under which those associations would carry causal meaning.
Both papers could have benefited from explicit causal theory and assumptions: the original study could have made transparent the conditions under which its findings would support causal conclusions, and the reproduction could have provided a constructive path forward by identifying specific causal threats and study designs that would address them.
Without such a framework, the reproduction---despite its valuable contributions to measurement and robustness---does not ultimately advance the field toward resolving the underlying causal question about programming languages and defects.

This impasse is revealing: Even though Ray et al.\ presented their results as correlational, the question that practitioners and researchers actually care about---\emph{should I choose Language A over Language B to reduce defects?}---is fundamentally causal.
When a study investigates a causal question using correlational methods but without an explicit framework for assessing what the findings can and cannot credibly claim, the resulting ambiguity invites precisely the kind of unproductive controversy that followed.

\paragraph{Downstream causal misinterpretation.}
The absence of an explicit causal framework has consequences beyond the original debate.
While \citet{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14} were careful to frame their findings as correlational, many downstream works citing the study have not maintained this distinction.
\citet{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19} noted in their reproduction that ``many subsequent works have cited the 2014 article and have asserted, without evidence, a causal link between the choice of programming language for a given task and the number of software defects.''
In a detailed analysis accompanying their counter-rebuttal, \citet{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1911-11894} reviewed citations to the original study and found that only 4 properly described the findings in terms of associations, while 26 incorrectly implied or asserted causal relationships.
This pattern---where a carefully framed correlational study is progressively interpreted causally by downstream work---illustrates a systemic problem that the framework proposed in this thesis directly addresses.
Had the original study included an explicit causal model articulating the assumptions under which its associations would or would not support causal interpretation, downstream authors would have had a clear basis for calibrating their claims.
The programming language debate thus serves as a compelling pedagogical example not only because of its intrinsic interest but because it vividly demonstrates how the absence of explicit causal reasoning creates an ``interpretive vacuum'' that downstream work fills with unjustified causal claims.
More recent work by \citet{DBLP:journals/tosem/FuriaTF24} has independently arrived at a similar diagnosis, demonstrating through structural causal models that purely associational and causal analyses of programming language data yield considerably different conclusions---further underscoring the need for accessible guidance on applying causal reasoning to software engineering research.

I now demonstrate how the four-step causal credibility assessment framework proposed in this thesis can move beyond this impasse.
The goal is not to criticize either Ray et al.\ or Berger et al.---the former's correlational framing was appropriate given their design, and the latter raised legitimate measurement and robustness concerns---but to show that applying explicit causal reasoning to a fundamentally causal question clarifies what existing evidence can support, where its limitations lie, and what specific study designs would yield stronger evidence.
This worked example is intended to serve as an accessible tutorial for software engineering researchers seeking to apply the framework to their own research problems.

\subsection{Applying the Causal Credibility Assessment Framework}
\label{sec:proposed-assessment}

\paragraph{Step 1: Make the causal theory explicit.}
Consistent with their correlational framing, \citet{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14} do not present an explicit causal model.
Their statistical approach---a negative binomial regression of defect-fixing commits on language, controlling for project age, project size, and number of developers---is designed to estimate conditional associations rather than causal effects.
This choice is defensible on its own terms, but it leaves an important question unanswered: What \emph{would} the data-generating process need to look like for the reported associations to reflect genuine causal effects from the choice of programming languages?

Making the causal theory explicit as a DAG (as developed in Figure~\ref{fig:pl-dag} in Chapter~\ref{chp:background}) helps answer this question.
The DAG encodes several confounders---Domain, Team Skill, Org Culture, and Codebase Age---each of which plausibly affects both language choice and defect proneness through independent pathways.
The back-door criterion (Section~\ref{sec:formalism}) shows that conditioning on all four would be necessary to block every confounding path.
Yet the covariates available in the regression model---project age, project size, and developer count---are at best crude proxies for some of these confounders and entirely miss others.
Specifically, the project age partially captures Codebase Age, and the developer count is a noisy proxy for Team Skill;  neither Domain nor Org Culture is measured in the regression model.

The absence of an explicit DAG had concrete consequences for the subsequent debate.
Without a shared visual representation of the assumed data-generating process, neither the original authors nor the reproduction team could identify precisely \emph{which} confounding paths remained open, leading to vague disagreements about whether the findings ``adequately controlled for confounders'' rather than productive engagement with specific threats.
A DAG would have channeled the debate into concrete questions---Is Team Skill adequately proxied by developer count? Is Domain a relevant confounder in this sample?---rather than the generic ``correlation versus causation'' impasse that ultimately resulted.

\paragraph{Step 2: Define the causal estimand, estimation method, and identifying assumptions.}
Ray et al.'s regression coefficient on language type is best understood as a conditional association: the average partial association between programming language and defect-fixing commit counts, conditional on the included covariates.
The authors do not claim this coefficient is causal, and their correlational framing is appropriate given the design.
However, the question that motivates the study and drives practitioner interest---whether choosing one language over another would reduce defects---is inherently causal.
The potential outcomes framework helps clarify the gap between the correlational estimate and the causal quantity of interest.

For the regression coefficient to carry a causal interpretation, the study would require the conditional ignorability assumption (Equation~\eqref{eq:cond-ignorability}): that conditional on project age, size, and developer count, language choice is as-if randomly assigned.
This assumption is implausible given the DAG in Figure~\ref{fig:pl-dag}.
Even if the included covariates perfectly measured the concepts they proxy for, they would fail to block the back-door paths through Domain (application domains differ across languages and independently affect defect rates), Team Skill (more experienced developers select into certain languages and independently write fewer bugs), and Org Culture (organizations with stronger quality cultures adopt certain languages and independently produce fewer defects).
Furthermore, the study's cross-sectional design limits identification to \emph{between-project} comparisons: Python projects are compared with Haskell projects, Java projects with Ruby projects.
The potential outcomes framework makes explicit why this is problematic for causal interpretation: The study estimates $E[Y | D = \text{Haskell}, X] - E[Y | D = \text{Python}, X]$, but this equals the causal effect only if selection bias (Equation~\eqref{eq:selection-bias}) vanishes conditional on $X$---which, given the unblocked back-door paths, it almost certainly does not.
Making these assumptions explicit does not invalidate the correlational findings, but it precisely delineates the conditions under which they would---or would not---support the causal conclusions that practitioners seek.

\paragraph{Step 3: Honestly acknowledge and mitigate limitations.}
Beyond the identification considerations, the study faces several data and measurement challenges that affect how strongly the findings---whether interpreted correlationally or causally---can be trusted.

First, the treatment---``programming language''---is operationalized using GitHub's dominant-language classification, which assigns each repository a single language based on byte counts.
\citet{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19} demonstrated that many repositories are polyglot, containing substantial code in secondary languages, and that reclassifying these repositories substantially changed the estimated effects.
This measurement error in the treatment variable violates the SUTVA requirement that treatment be well-defined: A repository classified as ``JavaScript'' that contains 40\% TypeScript code does not cleanly represent either language's effect.

Second, the outcome---``defect proneness''---is proxied by bug-fix commits identified through regular expression matching on commit messages.
This heuristic is noisy: Not all commits matching bug-fix patterns represent genuine defects, and not all defects are reflected in matching commits.
More critically, different language communities may follow different commit conventions.
If Haskell developers write more descriptive commit messages that are less likely to match bug-fix patterns, the observed association between Haskell and fewer ``bugs'' may partly reflect commit-message culture rather than actual defect rates.

Third, the study does not conduct sensitivity analysis to assess how strong unmeasured confounding would need to be to explain away the reported effects.
Tools such as $E$-values~\cite{vanderweele2017sensitivity} or Rosenbaum bounds~\cite{rosenbaum2002observational} would have provided readers with a quantitative assessment of the findings' robustness to unmeasured confounders, enabling more calibrated interpretation.

\paragraph{Step 4: Navigate alternative explanations.}
Because the study does not frame its investigation in causal terms, it does not systematically consider alternative data-generating processes that could produce the same observed associations.
An explicit causal framework would have enabled---and motivated---engagement with competing explanations, each of which implies a different DAG and different implications for the reported findings.
Four prominent alternatives illustrate how such engagement would work:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=15pt]
    \item \emph{Developer expertise as confounder.} If more skilled developers preferentially adopt functional and statically-typed languages and independently write fewer bugs, the observed association is (partly or wholly) an artifact of developer selection. This alternative implies a DAG where Language $\leftarrow$ Team Skill $\to$ Defect Proneness is an open back-door path. The cross-sectional design cannot distinguish this from a genuine language effect.
    
    \item \emph{Domain confounding.} Certain application domains (e.g., web front-ends, systems programming, data science) cluster around specific languages and have inherently different defect profiles due to differences in complexity, error handling requirements, and testing infrastructure. If domain assignment is correlated with language choice, the language coefficient absorbs domain effects. The study's covariates do not include any domain indicators.
    
    \item \emph{Ecosystem quality as alternative mechanism.} A critic might argue that the apparent benefit of statically-typed languages operates entirely through the maturity of the associated tooling ecosystem (linters, testing frameworks, IDE support) rather than through the type system itself. If ecosystem quality independently improves with language maturity, it acts as a confounder rather than a mediator, and conditioning on it (or failing to) affects the interpretation of the language coefficient.
    
    \item \emph{Reverse causality.} Quality-conscious teams may select ``safer'' languages \emph{because} they care about minimizing defects, reversing the assumed causal direction. Cross-sectional data cannot discriminate Language $\to$ Defects from Quality Consciousness $\to$ Language and Quality Consciousness $\to$ Defects.
\end{enumerate}

Each alternative implies distinct testable predictions---for instance, the developer-expertise alternative predicts that the language association should vanish when comparing the \emph{same developer's} contributions across languages, while the domain-confounding alternative predicts that controlling for application domain should substantially attenuate the association.
These predictions could, in principle, be tested empirically, but neither the original study nor the reproduction derives or examines them.
This is not a criticism of the original authors' stated goals---they did not set out to establish causation---but it illustrates how the absence of explicit causal reasoning left the field without a roadmap for resolving the disagreement that followed.

\subsection{Assessment Summary and Proposed Path Forward}

The four-step assessment is not meant as a criticism of Ray et al., whose correlational framing was appropriate for their study design.
Rather, it illustrates a broader pattern: When empirical software engineering research investigates fundamentally causal questions without explicit causal reasoning, three consequences tend to follow.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=15pt]
    \item \textbf{Ambiguous interpretation}: Without an explicit DAG, readers cannot determine which confounding paths the statistical controls address and which remain open. The resulting ambiguity invites both over-interpretation (treating associations as evidence of causation) and over-skepticism (dismissing all observational evidence as ``merely correlational'').
    \item \textbf{Limited identification}: A cross-sectional design with selection-on-observables provides the weakest form of evidence on the hierarchy discussed in Chapter~\ref{chp:background}. When the covariates are insufficient to block the back-door paths identified in the DAG, neither correlational nor causal interpretation of the regression coefficients is well-supported.
    \item \textbf{Unproductive debate}: Without testable predictions derived from competing causal structures, disagreements devolve into generic positions about ``correlation versus causation'' rather than progressing toward concrete evidence that would resolve the question.
\end{enumerate}

These consequences are not unique to Ray et al.---they reflect a common pattern in empirical software engineering research that investigates causal questions using correlational methods without explicit causal reasoning.
The tutorial paper proposed in this thesis uses this assessment to illustrate how the causal credibility assessment framework can constructively guide the design of improved studies.
Specifically, the cross-sectional design's inability to control for developer- and repository-level confounders (Consequence~2) motivates a \emph{panel fixed effects analysis} that exploits within-developer and within-repository variation, controlling for all time-invariant confounders without needing to measure them (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-fe}).
This proposed study serves as a concrete illustration within the tutorial of how the framework's diagnostic output translates into actionable research design improvements.

\section{Proposed Study: Panel Fixed Effects Analysis}
\label{sec:proposed-fe}

The credibility assessment reveals that the cross-sectional design used by Ray et al.\ cannot disentangle language associations from time-invariant developer and project characteristics that drive both language choice and defect rates.
To address this gap, I propose a panel fixed effects analysis that leverages longitudinal software repository data to control for unobserved heterogeneity at both the developer and repository levels.

\subsection{Research Design}

The key insight is that many developers and repositories work with multiple programming languages over time, creating \emph{within-unit variation} that can be exploited for identification.
Rather than comparing Haskell developers with Python developers (a between-unit comparison confounded by developer characteristics), this design compares the \emph{same developer's} contributions when working in different languages (a within-unit comparison that eliminates time-invariant developer effects).

Specifically, I propose estimating fixed effects models of the form:
\begin{equation}
\textit{Defects}_{ijt} = \alpha_i + \gamma_j + \lambda_t + \beta \cdot \textit{Language}_{ijt} + \Gamma' Z_{ijt} + \epsilon_{ijt}
\end{equation}
where $\textit{Defects}_{ijt}$ represents defect-related commits by developer $i$ in repository $j$ at time $t$; $\alpha_i$ represents developer fixed effects capturing time-invariant developer characteristics (skill level, coding style, attention to quality); $\gamma_j$ represents repository fixed effects capturing time-invariant project characteristics (complexity, domain, team culture); $\lambda_t$ represents time fixed effects capturing temporal trends; and $\textit{Language}_{ijt}$ indicates the programming language used.

This specification controls for confounders that are constant within developers or within repositories.
Referring to the DAG in Figure~\ref{fig:pl-dag}, developer fixed effects absorb the back-door path through Team Skill (to the extent that developer expertise is stable over the study period), and repository fixed effects absorb paths through Domain and Org Culture (to the extent that these are stable within repositories).
The identifying variation comes from developers who contribute to multiple languages or repositories that use multiple languages, enabling within-unit comparisons that are less susceptible to selection bias than the between-unit comparisons in Ray et al.

\paragraph{Data Construction.}
The analysis requires constructing a panel dataset of developer--repository--month observations with associated language and defect information.
I will operationalize this using the dataset shared by \citet{DBLP:journals/toplas/BergerHMVV19}, identifying developers who contribute to repositories in at least two distinct programming languages during the observation period.
Defect-related commits will be identified using the commit-message heuristics of \citet{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/RayPFD14}, supplemented by issue-linked commit analysis where available.
Language assignment at the commit level---rather than the repository level---avoids the polyglot misclassification problem.

\subsection{Expected Challenges and Limitations}

Fixed effects alone cannot address time-varying confounders.
A developer who switches from JavaScript to TypeScript may do so precisely because they are working on a more complex feature requiring stronger typing---in which case the language switch is confounded with feature complexity.
This limitation is intrinsic to fixed effects designs and cannot be resolved without additional identifying variation, such as quasi-experimental designs that exploit specific language transition events.

Second, the analysis requires sufficient within-unit variation: developers must contribute in multiple languages, and repositories must use multiple languages.
If the majority of developers specialize in a single language, the fixed effects estimator will have limited statistical power.
I will assess the available within-developer variation at the data construction stage and report the fraction of developers contributing to each pair of languages.

Third, the causal estimand in a fixed effects model is the \emph{within-unit} effect, which may differ from the population-average effect if language effects are heterogeneous.
Developers who work in multiple languages may be systematically different from single-language developers (e.g., more versatile, more senior), limiting the generalizability of findings.

\section{Expected Contributions}

The proposed work is expected to contribute in several ways.
First, by packaging the causal credibility assessment as an accessible tutorial with a familiar worked example, the proposed paper lowers the barrier for software engineering researchers to adopt explicit causal reasoning in their own work.
The programming language and defect proneness debate is widely known in the community, making it an effective pedagogical vehicle for demonstrating how the four-step framework can clarify the strengths and limitations of existing research and guide improved study designs.

Second, the causal credibility assessment of the programming language debate demonstrates a complementary use of the framework: applying it not to design new studies from scratch (as in Chapters~\ref{chp:pinning}, \ref{chp:fake-stars}, and~\ref{chp:cursor}) but to constructively engage with existing research, identifying specific gaps and articulating what would be needed to address them.

Third, the proposed panel fixed effects study provides new empirical evidence on a longstanding question, using methods that move beyond the correlational evidence in prior work by controlling for time-invariant developer and project characteristics that confound cross-sectional comparisons.
Regardless of whether the analysis finds that programming language affects defect proneness, the methodological contribution stands: the tutorial illustrates how to move from a diagnostic assessment of existing evidence to a concrete, improved study design.

Finally, the downstream citation analysis documents a systemic pattern---carefully framed correlational findings being progressively reinterpreted as causal by citing work---that motivates the need for explicit causal frameworks beyond this specific debate.
This analysis provides empirical support for the thesis argument that the absence of explicit causal reasoning in software engineering research has concrete, measurable consequences for how findings propagate through the literature.

\section{Summary}

This chapter has outlined a proposed tutorial paper that demonstrates how the causal credibility assessment framework developed in this thesis can serve as both a diagnostic tool for existing research and a design guide for future studies.
By applying the four-step framework to the programming language and defect proneness debate, the assessment revealed how investigating a fundamentally causal question without explicit causal reasoning---even when findings are carefully framed as correlational---leads to ambiguous interpretation, limited identification, and unproductive debate that the field struggles to resolve.
The downstream citation analysis further demonstrates a concrete consequence of this absence: carefully framed correlational findings are progressively reinterpreted as causal by citing work, creating an ``interpretive vacuum'' that an explicit causal framework would have prevented.

The proposed panel fixed effects study (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-fe}) illustrates how the framework's diagnostic output translates into actionable research design improvements, addressing the identification gap by exploiting within-developer and within-repository variation to control for time-invariant confounders.

Together with the completed empirical studies, the proposed work demonstrates the full scope of this thesis's contribution: that \emph{causal credibility assessments are feasible in empirical software engineering through an explicit articulation of causal theory and assumptions}.
The four-step process demonstrated throughout this thesis (1) guides the design of new studies to produce trustworthy evidence (Chapters~\ref{chp:pinning}, \ref{chp:fake-stars}, \ref{chp:cursor}), (2) enables constructive evaluation of existing research on fundamentally causal questions (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-assessment}), and (3) charts a principled path from identified limitations to improved study designs (Section~\ref{sec:proposed-fe}).
By packaging this framework as an accessible tutorial with a familiar worked example, the proposed work aims to lower the barrier for adoption and contribute to a broader shift toward explicit causal reasoning in empirical software engineering research.
