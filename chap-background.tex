\section{The Problem}

For centuries, researchers have been attempting to answer questions in the following form: In a population, does an intervention (or treatment) $X$ lead to a change in outcome $Y$? 

\subsection{The Potential Outcomes Framework}  

Mathematically speaking, for any entity $i$ in the population, let $Y_i^1$ denote the outcome of $i$ with intervention and $Y_{i}^0$ the outcome without, what is the average treatment effect $ATE = E[Y_{i}^1] - E[Y_{i}^0]$? 

The key problem blocking the computation of $ATE$ is that $Y_{i}^1$ and $Y_{i}^0$ cannot \emph{both} be measured without using a time machine.
To alleviate this, all causal inference methods seek to estimate $ATE$ unbiasedly from experimental or observational data under different assumptions.

This leads to the gold standard of randomized controlled trials, which are widely used in medicine today~\cite{bothwell2016assessing}.
However, the assumption almost always fails in observational data, prompting economists and social scientists to seek alternative approaches.

\subsection{Difference-in-Differences} 

One of the workhorse methods for causal inference in observational data is \emph{difference-in-differences} (DiD)~\cite{card1993minimum, cunningham2021causal}.
Its high-level idea is to estimate $ATE$ by comparing outcome $Y$ in a treatment group before and after intervention with changes in a control group \emph{over the same period}. 
Crucially, it makes a \emph{parallel trends assumption}---that treatment and control groups would have followed similar trajectories in the absence of intervention. 
One common setting of DiD estimation is that $N$ entities (containing both treatment and control) are observed over the same $T$ periods (i.e., \emph{panel data}~\cite{baltagi2008econometric}), but the intervention can happen at different times (i.e., \emph{staggered adoption}~\cite{athey2022design}).
In this setting, we can represent $ATE$ as the $\beta$ parameter in the following linear model:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:base-did}
    Y_{it} = \alpha + \beta D_{it} +\mu_i + \lambda_t + X'_{it}\Gamma + \epsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
Here $Y_{it}$ is the outcome variable for entity $i$ at time $t$; $D_{it}$ is a binary variable representing whether entity $i$ receives the treatment at $t$; $\mu_i$ and $\lambda_t$ are per-entity and per-time fixed effects; $X'_{it}$ are additional time-varying control variables; and $\epsilon_{it}$ is the error term.

Apart from $ATE$, researchers are also often interested in the longitudinal evolution of treatment effects (i.e., estimating different $ATE$s $1$ to $k$ time periods after intervention).
This is typically estimated using an extension of Equation~\ref{eq:base-did}, in the following form~\cite{clarke2021implementing}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:panel-event}
     Y_{it} = \alpha + \sum_{j=2}^{J}\beta_j(\text{Lead}~j)_{it} + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \gamma_k (\text{Lag}~k)_{it}+\mu_i + \lambda_t + X'_{it}\Gamma + \epsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
Here $\text{Lead}~j$ and $\text{Lag}~k$ are dummies to support the estimation of treatment effects $-J$ to $K$ time periods before and after the intervention.
The treatment effects of $\text{Lead}~j$ can be used as placebo tests, as a reliable estimation should not have significant treatment effects before the intervention. 
$\text{Lead}~1$ is intentionally omitted from the model to serve as the counterfactual baseline.

For many years, the econometric literature has been relying on the two-way fixed effect (TWFE) estimator for estimating the model parameters in Equation~\ref{eq:base-did} and Equation~\ref{eq:panel-event}.
Importantly, under both the parallel trend assumption and the treatment effect homogeneity assumption, a TWFE estimator equals a weighted average of all possible two-group/two-period DiD estimators in the data~\cite{goodman2021difference}.
However, the treatment effect homogeneity assumption is a strong assumption that may not hold in many settings (e.g., the intervention is only effective in the short term).
Recent studies show that the violation of this assumption may lead to ``forbidden comparisons'' in the TWFE estimator, biasing the estimated parameters and jeopardizing the validity of causal claims based on TWFE models~\cite{de2020two, athey2022design}.
As a result, new estimators are proposed to produce unbiased estimations of $ATE$s in the absence of the treatment effect homogeneity assumption~\cite{borusyak2024revisiting} and, more aggressively, with weaker parallel trend assumptions~\cite{callaway2021difference}.
In general, it is often advised to test multiple estimators and conduct sensitivity analysis to establish credible causal evidence for the problem of interest~\cite{baker2022much, roth2023s}. 

\subsection{Generalized Method of Moments}

While DiD can effectively estimate $ATE$ for a set of outcomes, they do not directly capture the temporal dynamics between the outcomes themselves. 
In our setting, it is well-known that velocity and quality interact with each other~\cite{DBLP:conf/sigsoft/ChengMCJGKZ022, besker2018technical}. 
To test such dynamic relationships and potential bidirectional causality, \emph{generalized method of moments} (GMM) is often used~\cite{hansen1982large}, which provides a framework for obtaining consistent causal estimates when variables are potentially endogenous (i.e., correlated with unobserved error terms). The key insight is to use \emph{instrumental variables}—variables correlated with the endogenous regressors but uncorrelated with the error term—to identify causal effects. 
In panel data, lagged values of variables often serve as natural instruments under the assumption that past values influence current values but are uncorrelated with current shocks~\cite{arellano1991some}.

For our analysis of the dynamic relationship between development velocity and code quality, we employ Arellano-Bond dynamic panel GMM~\cite{arellano1991some}, which is particularly suited for settings with: (1) dynamic dependence, where current outcomes depend on past outcomes; (2) potential bidirectional causality between variables; and (3) short time series with many entities. A panel GMM model can be specified as follows to test the causality direction $X_t \to Y_t$ while taking possible intervention $D$ into consideration:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dynamic-gmm}
Y_{it} = \alpha + \rho Y_{i,t-1} + \beta D_{it} + \gamma X_{it} + \mu_i + \lambda_t + Z'_{it}\Theta + \epsilon{it}
\end{equation}
where $Y_{i,t-1}$ captures persistence in the outcome, $X_{it}$ represents potentially endogenous regressors, $Z'_{it}$ stands for additional time-varying controls, and the remaining follows the specification in Equation~\ref{eq:base-did}. 
During estimation, historical values of $X_{it}$ (e.g., $X_{i,t-2}$) are used as instrumental variables.
A widely used efficient estimation method~\cite{windmeijer2005finite} of Equation~\ref{eq:dynamic-gmm} eliminates entity fixed effects $\mu_i$, which may correlate with the lagged dependent variable, with a first-order difference transformation.
During estimation, first-order difference transformation is often applied to eliminate entity-specific bias, and historical values of $X_{it}$ (e.g., $X_{i,t-1}, X_{i,t-2}, ...$) are used as instrumental variables.
Panel GMM models will complement our DiD analysis by decomposing the mechanisms through which Cursor adoption affects long-term development outcomes.
